# Composition Patterns & Originality Rules

Pick ONE structure type per image. Do not mix several.

## Structure types

### Workflow

For: input → process → output, content pipelines, automation chains.

Draw: input objects on the left, a crew member operating a strange contraption in the middle, output on the right. Yellow scribble line traces the flow. Labels name each stage.

### Before / After

For: messy vs. organized, manual vs. automated, stuck vs. flowing, anxious vs. calm.

Draw: chaos on the left (tangled yellow scribbles work well for chaos), calm order on the right, one crew member mid-transition. Labels: one word per side plus one on the turning point.

### Concept metaphor

For: a single big idea — a content garden, an idea press, a trust bridge, a knowledge well.

Draw: one memorable oversized object or contraption as the hero, a crew member operating it. Few inputs, one output. This is the most distinctive pattern — reach for it often.

### Character state

For: reader pain points, creator moods, tool overload, the journey from blocked to shipping.

Draw: 2–4 small vignettes of one crew member in different states, each with a one-or-two-word label. Fern is usually the star here.

### Layered method

For: frameworks, stacks, tiered systems, priority levels.

Draw: stacked boxes, shelves, or a tiered plant stand — hand-drawn and wobbly, never a formal pyramid. A crew member stacks, climbs, or waters the layers.

### Path / journey

For: idea to launch, learning roadmaps, user journeys, handoff sequences.

Draw: one winding charcoal path with 3–5 nodes, a crew member walking it (or Doodle running it), yellow scribbles marking detours. Labels on the nodes only.

### Mini comic

For: a real process with a twist, failure-to-success stories, relatable struggle.

Draw: 2–4 loose panels (hand-drawn borders or no borders), one action per panel. Speech bubbles allowed: "!!!", "...", or a scribble-tangle for confusion.

## Inventing an original metaphor

Reinvent the metaphor from the current article every time. Never reuse a previous output's composition. Three steps:

1. **Turn the abstract concept into a physical action**: pressing, sorting, planting, watering, fetching, delivering, untangling, stacking, weighing, filtering, stitching, harvesting.
2. **Turn the system into a charming low-tech object**: a flower press, a mailbox, a watering can, a clothesline, a market stall, a drawer chest, a gramophone, a loom, a garden bed, a wobbly ladder, a hand-cranked machine.
3. **Cast the right crew member to perform the action** (see `character-cast.md` casting rules). They operate the object; they don't pose next to it.

Use only 1–2 hero objects per image. Oversize them boldly.

## Anti-replication rules

The images in `assets/style-references/` calibrate texture, palette, and character design ONLY. Never copy their compositions:

- Penny striding with the giant pencil over her shoulder and a clipboard
- The full-crew group portrait / lineup with everyone posing for the camera (prefer a purposeful workshop-collab scene instead)
- Fern sitting cross-legged with the giant tea mug, open book, and scribble thought bubble
- Echo cross-legged beside the giant gramophone while Doodle lies with a paper scroll
- Margo skipping with the giant envelope and letters fluttering behind her
- Otto striding with the giant wrench over his shoulder and a toolbox
- Spark casually holding the glowing marigold arc disc with a floating drone beside him
- Milo peering through the giant magnifying glass following a footprint trail

Same rule for the skill's own previous outputs: a repeated theme still gets a fresh metaphor. "Publishing consistently" doesn't have to be Margo with envelopes twice — the second time it can be Penny pinning pages to a clothesline.
